In Billy Walters’ world, the final score of a game is often a "distraction" that doesn't reflect how good a team actually is. To find an edge, he uses a Power Rating system to determine what the "true" score should have been.Here is how you can break down the two main components of his "True Score" philosophy:1. Stripping Out "Luck" (Efficiency Metrics)Walters looks for teams that played better or worse than the scoreboard suggests. He focuses on metrics that are more predictable than points:Success Rate vs. Scoring: If a football team gains 500 yards but only scores 10 points because of two random fumbles, the public sees a "bad" offense. Walters sees an elite offense that had a "unlucky" day. He bets on them to regress to their high-yardage mean next week.Turnover Margin: Turnovers are notoriously "noisy" and random. A team that wins because of a +4 turnover margin is often overvalued the following week. Walters "recalculates" that game as if the turnovers hadn't happened to see who the superior team really was.High-Value Situations: He looks at 3rd-down conversion rates and Red Zone efficiency. If a team is scoring touchdowns on every Red Zone trip, they are likely overachieving and due for a "cooldown."2. Factoring in "The Human Element" (Contextual Factors)This is where Walters’ private information network gave him the biggest edge. He adjusted his ratings based on things the average bettor ignores:The "Travel Tax": It’s not just "away vs. home." He looked at how many time zones a team crossed, if they were on the tail end of a long road trip, or if they had to play in extreme altitude (like Denver).Schedule Spots: He targeted "Look-Ahead" games (a team playing a weak opponent right before a massive rivalry game) or "Let-Down" games (a team playing a weak opponent immediately after an emotional, hard-fought win).Hidden Injuries: Walters didn't just care if a Quarterback was out; he cared if the Left Tackle had a lingering turf toe. If a key "unseen" player is at 70% capacity, the whole team's efficiency rating drops, even if the "star" players are healthy.How to Apply ThisTo build your own version, you create a Power Rating (a number representing a team's strength) and update it weekly.Start with a Base Number: Give every team a rating from 1 to 100.The Adjustment: If Team A (Rating 85) beats Team B (Rating 70) by only 3 points, but Team A dominated every statistical category, you might increase Team A’s rating and decrease Team B’s, even though Team B "covered the spread."Find the Discrepancy: Compare your "True" spread (the difference between your two ratings) to the sportsbook's spread. If your math says the gap should be 10 points and the book says 6, you’ve found "Walters-style" value.
